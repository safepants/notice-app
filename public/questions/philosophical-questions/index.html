Philosophical Questions for Real Conversations

You don't need to have read Plato for these. They're the questions humans have been asking since the beginning — just framed in a way you can actually discuss over dinner, on a walk, or while playing a game with people you care about.

  1. 1What do you think happens when we die?
  2. 2If this is as good as it gets, would that be enough?
  3. 3Will you live a great life?
  4. 4What aspect of society do you reject?
  5. 5If you could get the whole world behind one idea, what would it be?
  6. 6What is one reason to be optimistic about the world today?
  7. 7"Most people feel more alone now than they did 10 years ago." Address the statement like a politician
  8. 8What do you think your life looks like from the outside?
  9. 9Name a belief you held a year ago that you've quietly dropped
  10. 10If you could have a conversation with any version of yourself — past or future — which one?
  11. 11If you could be remembered for one thing, what would it be?
  12. 12If you could ask the entire world one question and get an honest answer, what would you ask?
  13. 13If you could witness any moment in history — just watch, not change it — what would you pick?
  14. 14What's something you know is true that most people don't believe?
  15. 15What's one of the most underrated qualities in life?

frequently asked

How do you discuss philosophy without it getting pretentious?

Ask questions, don't make statements. 'What do you think happens when we die?' is philosophy. It's also just a question. The pretentiousness comes from having answers, not from asking.

Can philosophical questions work in casual settings?

That's where they work best. A bar, a bonfire, a late-night drive. Philosophy is just humans wondering about things together. The setting should be comfortable, not academic.
